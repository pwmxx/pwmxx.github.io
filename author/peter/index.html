November 7, 2019 by Peter Warwick-Mahoney

There‚Äôs been a number of news stories about the BERT update from Google.

(That seems like a non-brainer anyway ‚Äì content should always be useful and informative.)

But it was the beginning of the end for the concept of ‚Äòhow many keywords you can rank for‚Äô, or ‚Äòjust say this three times and you‚Äôll rank really highly‚Äô.

In the years following Google has made all kinds of similar small updates to improve that system. They‚Äôre committed to returning useful well-written, informative results; and they‚Äôve regularly made changes to their algorithm to further that aim.

The BERT SEO update

The BERT update really just pushes that to the next level. ‚ÄòPattern matching‚Äô for specific phrases isn‚Äôt enough anymore, not by a long shot.

Fortunately this is all in line with what I‚Äôve been saying for years ‚Äì don‚Äôt just write to specific words (whether they‚Äôre ‚Äòlong tail‚Äô or something else, the concept of expecting a certain amount of repetition to rock your SEO isn‚Äôt valid) ‚Äì rather write well thought out content that explains what you‚Äôre trying to say.

To give you an example. You‚Äôd think the key with my own site is to mention SEO a lot. That‚Äôs easy to do ‚Äì but harder to do in a way that makes it clear to the reader that I‚Äôm an authority on the subject, and even harder to impart useful information that might entice people back to my blog.

Simply repeating keywords is essentially keyword stuffing and Google knows it.

My recommendation for writing content hasn‚Äôt changed. Write to your audience, not to search engines. If you‚Äôre writing an about page then write about what you do and how you help people. Useful, natural words and phrases will come out of that anyway.

Your visitors ‚Äì and increasingly your search engine ranking ‚Äì will thank you for it.

Why context matters so much for SEO

As search engines continue to prepare for voice search being used more often (voice searches are usually quite conversational in their wording) context becomes more important.

To give a really basically example, let‚Äôs think about a site that may have been a review for a florist. Even if the content said something negative, for example ‚ÄúWhat a rubbish florist‚Äù, previously it would have helped rank for the search query ‚Äòflorist‚Äô. Increasingly Google can understand the negativity there and is less likely to present that site to someone who clearly wants to find a reputable local florist.

That really is a very basic example, but context is so important.

What needs changing for your WordPress SEO

As a WordPress SEO expert every change and update to SEO algorithms always comes back to the same question, ‚ÄúHow does this impact WordPress SEO?‚Äù The answer in this case is very little. Unlike structured data or other updates that necessitate coding changes to your site, this really is all about content.

Fortunately WordPress itself is all about content and the ease of editing or creating it ‚Äì so that‚Äôs a real boon. It‚Äôs easy to edit your site‚Äôs content to make it as contextual, human readable and useful as possible.

But there is no immediate need to jump in add or remove any plugins or systems.

Crawled ‚Äì currently not indexed

October 31, 2019 by Peter Warwick-Mahoney

This is a creative use of Google Search Console I‚Äôve come up with for some easy SEO wins

And it‚Äôs been working really well on all my testing sites.

Google Search Console (GSC) is Google‚Äôs official stats system for organic search, and has the most useful data about your site‚Äôs ranking, visits, clicks, etc.

They changed the interface for it some time ago and keep adding more data in it. One new page will show you all the pages in your site they‚Äôve crawled, but chosen not to include in search results.

So they know about these pages, but don‚Äôt think they‚Äôre worth including in their results pages. The effect of that is these pages are not actually helping with your SEO at all. From an SEO viewpoint they may as well not be there.

There are two main reasons I‚Äôve found why otherwise useful content is being ignored by Google:

  • It‚Äôs just too short
  • It‚Äôs not on-topic enough

Your content might just be too short

This is fairly self-explanatory. Unless you‚Äôve got 300+ words of text on a page it will often be ignored.

Your content might not be on-topic enough

The best way to explain this is with an example, let‚Äôs think about a digital marketing firm. Perhaps when they started they offered a variety of services from social media content to web development and SEO.

Well now they just focus on the latter. Their homepage is about it, all their services pages are too ‚Äì but they have old blog posts about their older services.

Google knows what a site‚Äôs core service offering is. And things that might fall outside that ‚Äì either because they‚Äôre just off topic or simply old and reflect what a business *used* to be about ‚Äì they‚Äôre likely to get ignored as well.

Re-purpose old content to get it indexed and working for your SEO!

Here‚Äôs my recommendation. Have a look through the list of crawled but not indexed pages and see if there are any you can fix.

Doing this myself I found any short pages I fleshed out, or outdated ones I re-tooled to reflect what I‚Äôm doing now ‚Äì every single one got picked up again by Google.

In some cases it was just a matter of adding an extra paragraph to an existing post. That‚Äôs not nearly as cumbersome as writing an entirely new one but essentially gets a whole extra page indexed.

Google Search Console & average position explained

August 5, 2019 by Peter Warwick-Mahoney

There are times your SEO results will look like the opposite of what you‚Äôre after.

Average Position is a bit of a weird metric. Here‚Äôs why.

Consider that graph for a moment. Taken directly from Google Search Console (Google‚Äôs official stats package for organic search ‚Äì and my recommendation for the tool you should use to get your ranking and other organic search results) they show two highlighted metrics;

  • Total impressions (in teal) and
  • Average position (in purple).

Impressions is the number of times a site was seen in search results. Average position is the average rank it was seen in.

(Google averages your rank across all impressions as a useful workaround to their personalisation they do when people are searching. Location, how often a user has searched for something before ‚Äì there are myriad ways Google skews results. Averaging them out is the best approach to find the most useful ranking to report.)

If you look at the lines on the graph though you‚Äôll see a number of places where the position appears to get worse right when the impressions become more successful. You‚Äôd think stats should improve hand-in-hand, not get worse as one improves. Right?

In reality, when impressions spikes it‚Äôs normal for the average position to drop. If a site suddenly has a lot more visibility, it usually means it‚Äôs being found for a bunch of terms and searches it wasn‚Äôt previously. A lot of those new terms are likely to be things sort of around the periphery of what the site is about, not just it‚Äôs core purpose. So it‚Äôs being found for a bunch of extra search terms.

The result of that is those new terms probably aren‚Äôt ranking terribly highly. They‚Äôre not the site‚Äôs main purpose, so won‚Äôt be ranking as highly as the normal terms the site has been successful for.

Which drags the average rank DOWN.

Not only is this good to know when interpreting your own results, but it serves as a reminder that stats aren‚Äôt simply the end of the story, it‚Äôs important to understand how they come about, and what their effect on each other is.
