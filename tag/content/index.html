Search Engine Optimisation (SEO): Blogging

May 21, 2013 by Peter Warwick-Mahoney

Continuing this series of posts, I want to say this very clearly. Blogging is the very best thing you can do for your search engine optimisation. If you are not blogging, you are ignoring the biggest factor in improving your placement in their listings.

Quite simply, if you put all the other tips and tricks aside, the real key to SEO is regular, original content.

It‚Äôs really simple. Google et al want to see content written by humans, for humans. So write some.

1. Regular
Search engines work out how often to scan your website and specific pages within that based on how often it has seen those change in the past.

If your pages are all fairly static (meaning there aren‚Äôt any elements on them that change regularly, like text or images) the search giants (Google, Bing, Yahoo!) will stop coming. Google might only index your site every few months.

There‚Äôs some massive missed opportunities there. Not only will your site be deemed to have a low Page Rank (not very important in the search results) but even if you do update with some new amazing, original content‚Äîit might not be noticed until it‚Äôs well out of date.

2. Original
Search Engines are clever. Google is adept at scanning text and knowing if it‚Äôs the sort of thing a human would read, rather than a bunch of words strung together to try to fool it. They also know very easily if your work is a copy and paste from somewhere else.

Apart from simply making money, search engines all have an ethos behind them. Google for example wants to see a helpful, information rich Internet that answers users‚Äô queries quickly and efficiently. So they rank sites they believe fit into that plan more highly than those that don‚Äôt.

If your site has too much content pasted from elsewhere, you do not fit into this ideal.

What does is original content. Something you‚Äôve written (or someone else has written exclusively on your behalf).

It‚Äôs not as hard as it sounds. Pick a subject that relates to your business. I do this with my blog all the time‚Äîand then in your head explain it as you would to one of your customers. Type out your side of the conversation, and you‚Äôre basically done.

3. Keywords
Before you even ask the question, the answer is yes. You do want to target keywords in your blogging. Bear in mind that as long as you‚Äôre writing on topic, they‚Äôll come out anyway. You want people to find you because of the sort of products and services you offer, so as long as you‚Äôre writing about the subject you sell, they‚Äôll flow naturally.

Rather than ensuring I type specific words, I do my best to hit a variety of terms.

The sentence you just read is a case in point‚Äîit would have seemed normal to use ‚Äúwords‚Äù in there twice; where I did and then again at the end of the sentence. By using ‚Äúterms‚Äù the second time, I‚Äôm improving my SEO because I‚Äôll be indexed for both ‚Äúwords‚Äù and ‚Äúterms‚Äù, rather than just one of them.

I do this all the time. Synonyms are are important but often overlooked aspect of SEO. A potential client might enter either ‚Äúsocial media‚Äù or ‚Äúsocial networking‚Äù for instance, and if that‚Äôs your industry you want them to find you regardless of the phrase they‚Äôre most familiar with.

Search engine optimisation is all about people‚Äîpeople finding you. Make sure you‚Äôre giving them what they need to do precisely that.

Search Engine Optimisation (SEO): Social integration

1. Backlinks
At its simplest, the explanation of a backlink is, well, simple. Search engines scan social networks and basically count all the links to articles and content on other sites. It‚Äôs worth noting that social backlinks are given a fairly low importance by the big search engines, so unsurprisingly a link to your post from an article on The Guardian website carries a lot more gravitas. More on that in the next point.

2. Photos/graphics/images
Before you share your article, make sure it has an image as part of it. Facebook and Linkedin both include a graphic from your content next to the link, and links with images get more clicks. Simple.

A word of advice here from personal experience‚Äîmake sure you have an image in place before you even try to share your content via Facebook once, even if it‚Äôs just a test. 3. Social networks

  • Twitter is a no-brainer. It‚Äôs short, simple, scanned by Google in near real-time, and again, even if you have very few interactions on that network the links to your content exist.
  • Linkedin is, for almost every professional business, a no-brainer. It‚Äôs where professionals find each other, discuss their industries, and again, if nothing else you‚Äôve got links back to your content.
  • Blogs should be a no-brainer. Their potential as social networking is often overlooked, but those comments sections are valuable. I include online newspapers and the link as blogs for this purpose; they‚Äôre very widely read. Here‚Äôs a top tip. Once a week, read a really popular blog about your industry.   • Tumblr is a great one too. We‚Äôre now in the realm of the networks I call ‚Äútime-allowing‚Äù, because while it‚Äôd be nice to hit up every network out there, I also hope you‚Äôre busy making money. But this is worth having a presence on, and sharing your ideas through.

4. Community
This is really important, because it‚Äôs not just backlinks that you want, it‚Äôs to have people taking about them, interacting, and ultimately sharing themselves. People don‚Äôt share content on social networks because they think it‚Äôs awesome, they share it because they think their friends will think it‚Äôs awesome. It‚Äôs an important distinction.

Part of this community building is for you to interact with your followers and subscribers. If someone looks like they want to get into a debate, then engage with that.
